# SUMMARY, CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

## 5.1 Summary of Major Findings

This study set out to address a specific and well-documented coordination gap experienced by students, young professionals, and middle-income households in urban Nigeria. As established in the literature reviewed in Chapter Two, existing ride-hailing and delivery platforms such as Uber, Bolt, and Chowdeck connect individual users to service providers but provide no mechanism for users with identical service intentions to discover one another and coordinate before a service is booked. This pre-service demand aggregation gap forces users in the same estate, campus, or neighbourhood to pay full individual prices for services they could share, resulting in duplicated costs that range from N1,500 to N5,000 per trip for users who have no convenient way to find and organise compatible group members (Ahmed, 2025). Informal coordination through WhatsApp groups, documented by Eze et al. (2021) as the most common workaround among Nigerian students, is hampered by information overload, absent threshold enforcement, manual cost calculation errors, and frequent coordination abandonment.

The major finding of this study is that MoveTogether successfully addresses this coordination gap through five core contributions. First, the platform provides a structured trip creation and discovery system that allows users to post group intentions across multiple activity categories, including Transport, Fitness, Adventure, Events, and Social activities, and allows other users to find and join those intentions through a filterable, searchable dashboard rather than a disorganized chat thread. Second, the join request approval mechanism introduces a trust layer that does not exist in informal messaging coordination, requiring trip creators to review and accept each participant individually before they are added to the travelers list. This two-step process directly addresses the trust barrier that Adegoke (2023) identified as the primary reason 67% of respondents avoided informal ride-sharing arrangements with strangers. Third, the automated cost-splitting calculation dynamically divides the total trip cost by the current number of travelers every time a new participant joins or leaves, providing a real-time financial commitment figure that all participants can see before the service is booked externally. This eliminates the manual calculation disputes documented by Akinwale and Omole (2022), who found that 63% of students regularly abandoned group ordering attempts due to the effort required. Fourth, the threshold management system monitors the travelers array against the maxTravelers field and automatically transitions the trip status from "active" to "full" when capacity is reached, preventing overbooking and giving the trip creator a clear signal to proceed with the actual service booking. Fifth, the real-time trip chat built on Socket.io provides a scoped, purpose-built communication channel for each trip group, supporting both text messages and coordinate-based location sharing, which directly replaces the unstructured WhatsApp threads that Hossain and Rahman (2020) found led to 52% of coordination attempts being abandoned entirely.

## 5.2 Conclusion

The aim of this study was to develop a web-based group coordination and trip-sharing system that enables users with similar destinations or service needs to form groups, coordinate activities, and share associated costs efficiently. Five specific objectives were stated in Chapter One to achieve this aim, and each is addressed individually below.

The first objective was to design and develop a platform that enables users to create and join group-based trips or activities based on shared destination, time, and purpose. This objective was fully achieved through the implementation of the trip creation and discovery modules. Authenticated users can create a trip by specifying a title, origin, destination, meeting point, departure date, transport type, category, maximum travelers, and total cost. Other users can discover these trips through the Explore page using keyword search, category filters, transport type filters, date filters, and sort options. Each trip card displays the current per-person cost and the fill ratio, providing immediate visibility into both the financial commitment and the availability of each trip. The system currently holds 21 frontend pages and components managing the full lifecycle from registration through trip completion, demonstrating the breadth of the platform's implementation.

The second objective was to implement a group formation mechanism that allows activities or trips to proceed only when a specified number of participants is reached. This objective was fully achieved through the threshold management system implemented in the trip controller. When the number of accepted travelers reaches the maxTravelers value set by the trip creator, the system automatically changes the trip status from "active" to "full". The trip creator can then manually advance the status to "confirmed" when the group is ready to proceed, or to "cancelled" if the coordination fails. The state machine enforces valid transitions across five possible states (active, full, confirmed, completed, cancelled), preventing illogical status changes and providing clear lifecycle governance for every trip. Real-time notifications delivered through Socket.io inform the trip creator and all travelers of status changes as they happen, which addresses the communication gap that Eze *et al.* (2021) documented as a major failure point in informal coordination.

The third objective was to design and implement coordination features that enable group members to agree on meeting points and activity execution plans. This objective was fully achieved through two complementary features. The Location Picker component, built using Leaflet and React-Leaflet, allows trip creators to visually select geographic coordinates for the origin, destination, and meeting point during trip creation, storing the precise latitude and longitude values in the Trip document. The TripChat system, built using Socket.io, provides each trip with a dedicated real-time messaging room that is accessible only to participants of that specific trip. The chat supports both text messages and location sharing, where a user can send their current coordinates as an interactive map element visible to all group members. The chat is locked for writing once a trip is completed or cancelled, preserving the coordination record.

The fourth objective was to develop a cost-sharing module that provides an equitable method for distributing shared expenses among participants. This objective was fully achieved through the automated equal-split calculation implemented across the trip creation, join, leave, and remove traveler endpoints. The per-person cost is always computed as the totalCost divided by the current number of travelers, and this figure is returned by the backend on every trip fetch so the frontend always displays an accurate, up-to-date cost split. The calculation updates automatically when any participant joins or leaves, requiring no manual input from any user at any stage of the coordination process.

The fifth objective was to evaluate the effectiveness and usability of the proposed system in improving group coordination and cost sharing among users. This objective was achieved through functional testing of all core modules across the five development sprints, with results presented in Chapter Four through seven figure references covering the authentication flow, the dashboard, the Explore page, the Trip Detail page, the trip chat, the profile page, and the admin dashboard. Each result was discussed in relation to the specific literature finding it addresses, demonstrating the system's capability to resolve the documented limitations of informal coordination tools.

In conclusion, MoveTogether demonstrates that a purpose-built web platform can replace the informal and ineffective WhatsApp-based coordination workflows that Nigerian students and young professionals currently rely on for shared transportation and delivery services. By automating the three most friction-prone elements of group coordination (finding compatible participants, calculating fair cost splits, and managing group capacity), the platform removes the barriers that prevent successful cost-sharing among people with identical service needs. If adopted within a university campus or residential community, MoveTogether has the potential to reduce per-person service costs significantly, given that the literature consistently shows that users who successfully coordinate shared rides and deliveries save between 30% and 60% on per-person expenses (Okonkwo and Eze, 2022; Kim *et al.*, 2019).

## 5.3 Recommendations

Based on the findings and outcomes of this study, the following recommendations are made. The first set of recommendations concerns future improvements to MoveTogether itself, followed by broader recommendations for developers, policymakers, and future researchers working in the area of digital coordination platforms for developing economies.

For MoveTogether specifically, the most important near-term improvement is the integration of a real payment processing system. The current implementation calculates per-person cost splits accurately but leaves the actual transfer of money between participants entirely to external bank apps. A future version of the platform should integrate a payment gateway such as Paystack or Flutterwave to allow the trip organizer to collect payments from all participants directly within the platform before the external service is booked. This would eliminate the most significant remaining source of coordination failure, which is the inability to hold participants financially accountable for their committed share before the trip organizer makes the external booking on their behalf.

The second improvement recommended for the system is the integration of real-time GPS tracking for active trips. The current implementation stores static origin, destination, and meeting point coordinates at trip creation time and supports manual location sharing through the chat. A production deployment should integrate the Geolocation API with live location updates to allow all trip participants to see each other's real-time positions on an interactive map during the pre-departure gathering phase, significantly improving punctuality and reducing the no-show problem that Eze *et al.* (2021) identified as a major failure point in informal coordination.

The third recommendation is the development of a direct API integration layer with major Nigerian service providers, including Bolt, Uber, Chowdeck, and GIG Logistics. This would allow the trip organizer, after their MoveTogether group reaches the confirmed threshold, to initiate the external booking directly from within the platform rather than switching to a separate app. This integration would close the last manual step in the coordination workflow and make the end-to-end journey from "identifying a shared need" to "executing the shared service" entirely contained within a single platform.

For developers and entrepreneurs in the Nigerian digital services space, this study recommends serious consideration of the pre-service demand aggregation model as an underexplored layer of opportunity in the local market. The existing platforms in Nigeria compete primarily at the service delivery layer, matching users to providers. This study demonstrates that a significant market exists one step earlier in the user journey, connecting users with identical intentions before a provider is engaged, and that this layer can be addressed with a relatively simple web platform built entirely from open-source tools at zero direct cost.

For researchers in the area of digital coordination and the sharing economy in Africa, this study recommends that future work investigate user adoption patterns for dedicated coordination platforms among Nigerian university students using quantitative survey methods with larger sample sizes. The studies reviewed in Chapter Two, including Ogunleye and Adeyemo (2020) and Adegoke (2023), provide strong evidence of demand for such platforms but stop short of evaluating actual adoption behavior when a functional platform is made available. A longitudinal study of MoveTogether usage patterns within a specific university campus would generate empirically grounded data on adoption rates, coordination success rates, average cost savings per trip, and the social dynamics of trust formation between strangers on a shared-activity platform. Such findings would make a meaningful contribution to the growing body of scholarship on collaborative consumption in sub-Saharan Africa.
