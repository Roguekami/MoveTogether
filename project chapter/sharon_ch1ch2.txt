Despite the rapid growth of platforms like Uber, Bolt, Jumia Food, and Chowdeck, these services primarily match individual users with service providers rather than enabling users to discover and coordinate with other users who share identical service intentions. This gap results in duplicated transactions, repeated delivery fees ranging from ₦1,500 to ₦3,000 per trip, missed economies of scale, and inefficient resource utilisation. This study addresses the problem by designing, implementing, and evaluating MoveTogether, a web-based group coordination platform that enables users to create and join group intentions (Trips) across four service types: Drive (shared commercial rides), Food (group food ordering), Dispatch (shared courier services), and Trek (group activities such as hiking). The platform incorporates user authentication, trip creation and discovery, automated equal-split cost calculation, threshold-based trip status management (Open, Full, Confirmed), and basic group coordination features.Table of ContentsTitle PageCertificationDedicationAcknowledgementsAbstractTable of ContentsList of TablesList of FiguresList of Abbreviations CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION1.1 Background to the Study1.2 Statement of the Problem1.3 Aim and Objectives of the Study1.4 Scope of the Study1.5 Significance of the Study1.6 Limitations of the Study1.7 Definition of Key Terms 2.4 Related Works (Empirical Review)    2.4.1 Historical Context: Transportation and Coordination Problems    2.4.2 Ride-Sharing and Ride-Hailing    2.4.3 Food Delivery and Group Ordering    2.4.4 Dispatch and Logistics Consolidation    2.4.5 Group Coordination and Cost-Splitting Tools    2.5 Summary of Literature Review2.6 Research GapCHAPTER ONEINTRODUCTION1.1 Background to the StudyThe sharing economy, also known as collaborative consumption, has become one of the defining features of the digital age. It operates on the principle that individuals can gain greater value by sharing access to goods and services rather than owning them exclusively. This model relies heavily on mobile technologies, real-time data, GPS tracking, and digital payment systems to connect users efficiently  Botsman and Rogers, 2010).Globally, platforms such as Uber, Airbnb, and BlaBlaCar demonstrate how demand aggregation and peer-to-peer coordination can reduce costs, improve resource utilization, and minimize environmental impact through reduced duplication of services. In developing regions like Africa, the sharing economy offers even stronger potential to tackle challenges such as high living costs, inadequate public infrastructure, and economic pressures on urban populations (Mike et al., 2024).In Nigeria, digital service adoption has grown rapidly, especially in major cities including Lagos, Abuja, and Port Harcourt. The ride-hailing sector has expanded significantly due to rising smartphone penetration, urbanization, poor public transport systems, and increasing fuel prices. The Nigerian ride-hailing market was valued at approximately USD 276–303 million in recent estimates, with projections to reach around USD 477 million by 2029 (Statista, 2025). On a broader scale, the African ride-hailing market stood at USD 2.53 billion in 2025 and is expected to grow steadily (Mordor Intelligence, 2026). Major players such as Bolt, Uber, and inDrive dominate the space, providing millions of daily trips and contributing meaningfully to the economy. For instance, Uber alone contributed an estimated ₦34 billion to Nigeria’s economy in 2023 through driver earnings, rider time savings, and related economic activities (Public First, 2025).The online food delivery sector has experienced even faster growth. The market reached USD 1,037–1,143 million in 2024/2025 and is projected to expand to USD 2.5–2.733 billion by 2033–2034, with a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 9.86–10.3% (IMARC Group, 2026). Local platforms like Chowdeck have scaled impressively, surpassing one million monthly orders and recording over 40,000 daily orders in late 2025. Dispatch and last-mile logistics services have similarly proliferated alongside the rise of e-commerce.Despite this impressive digital growth, end users continue to face notable challenges. Ride fares and delivery fees remain relatively high for many Nigerians, particularly students, young professionals, and middle-income households contending with inflation and rising living costs. A typical short ride in Lagos can cost ₦2,000–5,000, while food delivery fees often range from ₦1,500–3,000 or higher, even for modest orders. Dispatch services charge full fees for single packages, and group outings or treks become expensive when transport or guides are hired by only a few people (Ahmed, 2025).In densely populated residential estates, university campuses, and neighborhoods, multiple individuals frequently share nearly identical needs at the same time,, traveling to the same destination, ordering meals from the same restaurant, sending packages to the same area, or planning group activities. Yet, without an effective digital coordination tool, each person acts independently. This leads to duplicated efforts: several separate ride-hailing trips on the same route, multiple food deliveries from one restaurant to the same estate, or repeated courier dispatches to the same location. The outcome is higher per-person costs, inefficient use of vehicles and riders, increased traffic, and avoidable environmental strain.Traditional ride-hailing and delivery platforms primarily match individual users with service providers (drivers, restaurants, or couriers). They rarely facilitate matching between users who have the same intention before the service is purchased. This missing pre-service demand aggregation layer results in financial inefficiency and missed opportunities for collective savings.MoveTogether was conceived to fill this important gap. It is a web-based group coordination platform that enables users to create or join “group intentions” referred to as “Trips” for shared services. By connecting people with identical needs in terms of time, place, and activity, MoveTogether allows participants to reach a participation threshold, automatically calculate fair cost splits, and have one member execute the service on behalf of the group. This approach converts individual consumption into collaborative action, lowering per-person expenses while fostering community interaction and more efficient resource use.1.2 Statement of the ProblemEven though ride-hailing apps like Bolt and Uber, alongside food delivery services, are popular in cities like Lagos and Abuja, with markets valued at USD 253–1,143 million in 2024/2025 (Statista/IMARC, 2025/2026), users still pay excessively high per-person costs. In the same estates or neighborhoods, people often share identical needs at the same time: traveling to one destination, ordering from the same restaurant, sending packages to the same area, or joining group treks. Without an easy digital platform for pre-booking coordination, everyone acts alone, paying full ride fares, separate delivery fees (₦1,500–3,000), or hiring transport/guides for tiny groups. This wastes money, overburdens riders with duplicate trips, worsens traffic, and deters group activities amid rising living costs. Existing apps connect users only to providers, not peers; WhatsApp groups lack organized cost-splitting or thresholds. Thus, no simple web platform exists for creating/joining "group plans" across rides, food, dispatch, and treks to aggregate demand, split costs fairly, and execute efficiently.1.3 Aim and Objectives This chapter reviews literature and systems relevant to MoveTogether, a web-based group coordination platform for shared rides, food orders, dispatch deliveries, and group treks.The review follows this structure: Conceptual foundation, Theoretical framework, Existing systems, synthesis and Research gaps. Literature spans 2010–2026, including peer-reviewed journals, conference papers, industry reports, and platform documentation.2.2 Related Works This section reviews both empirical research studies and existing commercial systems relevant to MoveTogether. The review begins with the historical context of transportation and coordination problems, then examines academic studies on ride-sharing, food delivery, dispatch, and group coordination.The problem of high individual transportation costs and inefficient coordination is not new. Scholars have documented these challenges for decades. Santos et al. (2010) identified that urban transportation inefficiencies, including underutilised vehicle capacity and duplicated trips, contribute significantly to traffic congestion and environmental degradation. The authors estimated that private vehicles in urban areas operate at only 1.2 passengers per trip on average, representing substantial unused capacity.In the Nigerian context, Ogunleye and Adeyemo (2020) conducted a survey of 450 university students in Lagos and Ibadan and found that 78% of respondents reported spending more than 30% of their monthly allowance on transportation. The study further revealed that 62% of students had attempted to coordinate shared rides informally, primarily through WhatsApp groups and word-of-mouth, but cited difficulties in finding compatible companions and agreeing on cost splits as major barriers.Similarly, Adegoke (2023) investigated ride-sharing adoption among young professionals in Lagos and Abuja. The study, which surveyed 320 participants, found that cost reduction was the primary motivator for ride-sharing, with 84% of respondents indicating they would use a dedicated ride-sharing platform if available. However, the study also identified trust concerns (67% of respondents) and coordination difficulty (59% of respondents) as significant barriers to informal ride-sharing arrangements.These foundational studies establish that: (a) transportation costs are a significant burden for students and young professionals in Nigeria, (b) informal coordination attempts are common but ineffective, and (c) there is demonstrated demand for a dedicated coordination platform. Several empirical studies have examined ride-sharing and ride-hailing platforms, their adoption patterns, and their limitations.Chen et al. (2017) conducted a mixed-methods study of UberPool users in San Francisco, combining survey data (N=1,200) with interview data from 45 frequent users. The study found that while users appreciated the cost savings of shared rides, many expressed frustration with the lack of control over who they shared with and the unpredictability of pickup times. The authors recommended that future ride-sharing systems incorporate user-controlled group formation features a recommendation that directly supports MoveTogether's design.Rayle et al. (2016) compared ride-hailing users with traditional taxi users in San Francisco. Using survey data from 380 ride-hailing users and 290 taxi users, the study found that ride-hailing users prioritised convenience and shorter wait times, while cost sensitivity was the primary factor for taxi users. The study concluded that ride-hailing had not significantly reduced single-occupancy vehicle trips, suggesting that shared ride options needed to be more accessible and affordable.In the Nigerian context, Okonkwo and Eze (2022) developed and evaluated a mobile application for shared transport in Lagos. The prototype was tested with 50 users over four weeks. The study found that 72% of participants saved between 40% and 60% on transport costs when using the shared ride feature. However, the study noted that user adoption was hindered by the lack of a cost-splitting feature and the absence of pre-trip group formation, leading to mismatched expectations among co-riders. Food delivery has grown significantly, but group ordering remains underexplored in academic literature.Kim et al. (2019) studied group-buying mechanisms for food delivery in South Korea. The researchers conducted a controlled experiment with 240 participants, comparing individual ordering versus group ordering with cost-splitting. The study found that participants in the group ordering condition spent 35% less on delivery fees on average and reported higher satisfaction with the ordering experience. However, the study noted that group ordering required additional coordination effort, suggesting the need for dedicated coordination tools.Olaleye et al. (2021) examined food delivery adoption in Lagos, Nigeria, using survey data from 500 respondents. The study found that delivery fees were the most significant barrier to frequent use, with 71% of respondents indicating they would order more frequently if delivery fees were reduced. The study also found that 43% of respondents had participated in informal group ordering through WhatsApp or workplace chat groups, but 68% of these reported difficulties with payment collection and order tracking.Akinwale and Omole (2022) specifically studied group food ordering among students at the University of Lagos. The study surveyed 300 students living in university hostels and found that 58% had participated in group food orders, primarily to reduce delivery fees and meet minimum order requirements. However, the study identified coordination fatigue as a major issue, with 63% of participants reporting that the effort required to organise group orders often discouraged them from doing so regularly. Dispatch and courier consolidation has been studied primarily in the context of last-mile logistics.Wang et al. (2016) studied package consolidation strategies for urban logistics. Using simulation modelling with data from 10,000 daily deliveries in a major city, the study found that consolidating packages from multiple senders to the same destination area reduced delivery costs by an average of 42% and reduced vehicle trips by 38%. The study recommended the development of platforms that enable senders to discover consolidation opportunities.In the African context, Moyo and Ndlovu (2020) studied informal dispatch consolidation in Harare, Zimbabwe. Through interviews with 45 dispatch riders and 120 customers, the study found that informal consolidation (riders carrying packages from multiple customers in one trip) was already occurring but without any structured platform. Customers reported saving between 30% and 50% when their package was consolidated but expressed frustration with the lack of transparency and tracking.Nwagwu and Adebayo (2021) examined dispatch services in Lagos, Nigeria, focusing on the potential for cost-sharing among multiple senders. The study surveyed 200 small business owners who regularly use dispatch services. The findings showed that 74% of respondents would be willing to consolidate their packages with others going to the same area if a platform existed to facilitate this, and 68% would pay a small fee for such a coordination service.The effectiveness of digital tools for group coordination has been studied across multiple contexts.Tauscher and Laudien (2018) studied the adoption of cost-splitting applications (primarily Splitwise) among young adults in Germany. The study surveyed 350 university students and found that 81% of respondents had used a cost-splitting app, and 92% of those reported that the app reduced financial disputes within their social groups. However, the study noted that cost-splitting apps were used exclusively within pre-existing groups, not for discovering new group opportunities.Hossain and Rahman (2020) investigated the use of WhatsApp groups for coordinating shared services in Dhaka, Bangladesh. The study conducted interviews with 60 participants across 12 WhatsApp groups used for ride-sharing and group purchasing. The findings revealed that while WhatsApp was accessible and familiar, its unstructured nature led to information overload (79% of participants), missed opportunities (64%), and abandoned coordination attempts (52%). The study recommended dedicated platforms with structured trip formats and automated features.In the Nigerian context, Eze et al. (2021) evaluated the use of social media for group coordination among students at five Nigerian universities. The study surveyed 650 students and found that WhatsApp was the most commonly used tool for coordinating shared rides (67%), group food orders (54%), and shared dispatch (32%). However, 73% of respondents reported experiencing difficulties with these informal methods, including payment disputes, miscommunication, and no-shows.
