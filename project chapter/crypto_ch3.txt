CHAPTER THREEMETHODOLOGY3.1 Adopted MethodologyAgile Software Development methodology was chosen for the design and implementation of Crypto-Tax NG. Agile Software Development methodology is an approach to developing software iteratively and incrementally that places great emphasis on adaptability, continuous delivery, and collaboration at all levels of software development life cycle (Sommerville, 2016). In contrast to the classical Waterfall Model that mandates a very linear sequential progression in which one phase cannot begin until the previous one is completed in totality, Agile methodology enables overlapping of multiple phases (Pressman and Maxim, 2020). The Agile methodology was selected for this project for three specific reasons. First, Crypto-Tax NG spans several independent but deeply interconnected technical modules, which include a FIFO-based calculation engine, a multi-format CSV parsing pipeline, a P2P rate aggregation service, a progressive tax engine, and a PDF report generator. These components required continuous integration testing to ensure that changes to one module did not break others. A fixed, single-delivery model would have made this level of ongoing validation impossible. Second, the regulatory environment that this system operates in changed significantly during the development period, with the Nigeria Tax Act 2025 coming into effect on January 1, 2026, and replacing the Finance Act 2023 flat Capital Gains Tax rate with a progressive Personal Income Tax bracket system. The Agile framework allowed the tax calculation engine to be updated mid-development without requiring the entire project to be redesigned from scratch. Third, the target users of this platform are retail P2P traders who required usable, testable features at each stage of the project, rather than waiting for a completed system at the end. This aligns with the core Agile principle of delivering working software as the primary measure of progress (Beck et al., 2001).The development process was structured into five iterative sprints. Sprint One covered project setup, database configuration, and user authentication. Sprint Two implemented the wallets module and the CSV upload pipeline with the sniff-and-dispatch parsing engine. Sprint Three built the P2P rate aggregation service, which includes its seven-layer fallback chain from Redis to CoinGecko. Sprint Four implemented the FIFO calculation engine and the dual tax rate logic. Sprint Five covered the PDF report generator and system-wide integration testing. This sprint-based approach follows the Scrum framework as defined by Schwaber and Sutherland (2020).3.2 Analysis of the Existing SystemThe existing system selected for analysis is TaxPro Max, the official electronic tax filing and remittance platform operated by the Federal Inland Revenue Service (FIRS) of Nigeria. TaxPro Max was launched in 2021 as a unified digital gateway for federal tax obligations, covering Company Income Tax, Value Added Tax, Pay As You Earn, and Capital Gains Tax. Peter (2023) documented its initial impact on the formal corporate sector and found that the platform drove a 131% surge in Petroleum Profit Tax collection and a 62% improvement in VAT remittance within its first two years of operation, which established it as a genuinely effective tool for registered corporate filers.Under the Finance Act 2023 and the subsequent FIRS Information Circular issued in 2024, cryptocurrency traders operating in Nigeria are required to declare and remit Capital Gains Tax on profits from digital asset disposals through the TaxPro Max portal. The platform accepts a Tax Identification Number, a calculated net gain figure, and a remittance payment. On the surface, this appears to provide a complete compliance pathway for crypto traders. In practice, however, the platform's architecture exposes a fundamental structural gap when applied to this use case.TaxPro Max is what Dagunduro et al. (2025) described as a receiver-passive system. The portal is built to accept pre-calculated final figures submitted by professional accountants and large corporate tax departments. It does not contain any built-in engine for reading raw transaction files, applying First-In-First-Out accounting across multiple wallets and exchanges, converting historical P2P rates, or computing the taxable gain itself. A retail P2P trader who logs into TaxPro Max to declare their crypto Capital Gains Tax is presented with a blank input field that demands a final profit number, with zero tools provided to calculate that number. Fabian and David (2021) confirmed this structural limitation in their empirical study of TaxPro Max's adoption impact, finding that improvements in compliance were heavily concentrated among large registered companies that employed dedicated tax compliance staff, while self-employed individuals in the informal sector showed no measurable change in compliance rates after the platform was introduced.The process flow of TaxPro Max as it applies to a Nigerian crypto trader is illustrated in Figure 3.1 below./[Figure 3.1: Process flow of the existing system (TaxPro Max, FIRS)]3.2.1 Limitations or Drawbacks of the Existing SystemThe following limitations of TaxPro Max are identified in relation to the specific compliance needs of Nigerian P2P cryptocurrency traders:•  No built-in calculation engine: TaxPro Max accepts only pre-calculated tax figures. It does not provide any tool for computing Capital Gains from raw transaction data. A retail trader with hundreds of P2P trades across multiple exchanges has no mechanism within the platform to determine their taxable gain, which means the portal cannot be used without an external calculation system.•  No support for First-In-First-Out accounting: The FIRS mandated FIFO as the required cost basis method for digital assets in its 2024 operational guidelines. TaxPro Max has no FIFO engine, no cost basis tracking, and no lot matching capability. Traders who are required to apply FIFO across years of historical trades cannot do so using the portal alone.•  No P2P rate integration: TaxPro Max does not reference parallel market exchange rates, which are the rates at which Nigerian P2P traders actually conduct their transactions. As documented by Abu and Ohiaeri (2023), Nigerian cryptocurrency transactions are priced at parallel market rates that diverge from the official CBN NAFEM rate by an average of 81.7%. A platform that ignores this divergence cannot produce accurate cost basis or proceeds figures for Nigerian traders.•  No CSV or blockchain data ingestion: The platform cannot read or process the raw CSV files exported by cryptocurrency exchanges such as Binance, Quidax, Luno, or Bybit. Traders must manually extract relevant figures from their transaction history before the platform becomes usable, which introduces a significant risk of human error across potentially hundreds or thousands of transactions.•  No support for retail self-assessment: TaxPro Max was designed for professionally assisted filers, not for individual self-assessment by financially literate but technically unsophisticated retail traders. Templars Law (2024) characterised this as the platform assuming a level of financial and technical sophistication that is simply not representative of the typical Nigerian crypto user.3.3 Analysis of the Proposed SystemCrypto-Tax NG is a web-based cryptocurrency tax compliance system designed specifically for Nigerian P2P traders operating under the regulatory framework established by the Finance Act 2023 and the Nigeria Tax Act 2025. The system resolves every limitation identified in TaxPro Max by providing an end-to-end calculation pipeline that accepts raw exchange CSV files as input and produces a FIRS-compliant tax summary as output, without requiring the user to perform any manual calculations at any stage.The system authenticates users through a JWT-based authentication flow and supports a custom user model that stores Nigerian-specific fields including Tax Identification Number, phone number, and trader classification. Authenticated users link their exchange accounts through the wallets module, which supports exchange wallets, personal wallets, and hardware wallets. Transaction data is uploaded via a sniff-and-dispatch CSV parsing engine that automatically detects the format of any uploaded file from Binance, Bybit, Quidax, Luno, Roqqu, or Busha and normalises every row into a universal ledger schema. The FIFO calculation engine then processes all normalised transactions in strict chronological order, matches sales against purchases using a queue-based algorithm, and applies the correct tax rate based on the tax year being assessed. For tax years up to and including 2025, the system applies the 10% flat Capital Gains Tax rate established by the Finance Act 2023. For tax year 2026 and beyond, the system applies the progressive Personal Income Tax brackets introduced by the Nigeria Tax Act 2025. The final output is a PDF report formatted for manual entry into TaxPro Max, which includes a gain and loss summary, a FIFO matching table, and a tax liability breakdown.3.3.1 Preliminary DesignThe proposed system uses a client-server architecture consisting of a React 18 web frontend, a Django 5.0 REST API backend, a PostgreSQL 18 relational database, and a Redis caching layer. The frontend communicates with the backend exclusively through authenticated REST API calls using JWT Bearer tokens. The backend exposes all functionality through Django REST Framework viewsets and is organised into six independent Django applications. These applications cover users, wallets, transactions, pricing, calculations, and reports. Each application handles a distinct domain of the compliance workflow and exposes its own URL endpoints.The P2P rate aggregation service operates through a multi-tiered rate aggregation engine. When a historical rate is required for a given asset and date, the system checks an in-memory cache first, queries the Redis cache, queries the P2PRateHistory database table, calls the Binance historical klines API for the historical USD price, and then falls back to the CoinGecko historical API. It multiplies the resulting USD price by the monthly parallel market rate from the historical lookup table and saves the result to prevent repeated external API calls for the same date. For live USDT/NGN parallel market rates, the engine also queries the Luno Orderbook API which provides real-time NGN pricing directly from active Nigerian exchange orders. This architecture ensures that every rate lookup uses the actual P2P parallel market rate rather than an official CBN rate. This directly addresses the phantom gain problem identified by Bello (2024).The high-level architectural overview of the proposed system is illustrated in Figure 3.2 below./The core processing algorithm operates as follows. A user uploads a CSV file exported from their exchange. The sniff-and-dispatch engine reads the first row of the file, identifies its format by checking for signature column headers, and routes each row to the appropriate sub-parser. The sub-parser normalises the row into the universal Transaction schema and stores it in the database. The pricing service then enriches any transactions that lack NGN values by fetching historical rates through the seven-layer fallback chain. The FIFO engine processes all transactions in chronological order, maintaining a purchase queue per asset, and matches each sale against the oldest available lots until the sale quantity is exhausted. For each matched sale that falls within the requested tax year, a CapitalGain record is created with the proceeds, cost basis, and gain or loss in NGN. The tax engine then aggregates all CapitalGain records for the tax year, applies the appropriate tax rate, and stores the result in a TaxReport object. The report generator reads the TaxReport and produces a PDF document formatted for TaxPro Max submission.3.3.2 Proposed System JustificationThe proposed system is superior to TaxPro Max as a compliance tool for retail crypto traders on four specific dimensions. This directly resolves the 61.3% average deviation identified by Bello (2024).•  Full automation of the FIFO process: The system removes the need for any manual calculation by the trader. A user who uploads their exchange CSV files receives a complete, accurate FIFO-based tax report without performing a single manual computation. This addresses the control belief barrier identified by Ajzen (2019) as the primary compliance obstacle for Nigerian crypto traders.•  Multi-exchange support: The system normalises transaction data from six Nigerian-relevant exchanges into a single universal ledger, allowing traders who operate across multiple platforms to produce a consolidated tax return from one system.•  Dual tax law compliance: The system correctly applies the Finance Act 2023 flat 10% Capital Gains Tax rate for historical tax years up to 2025 and the Nigeria Tax Act 2025 progressive Personal Income Tax brackets for the 2026 tax year onwards, ensuring that reports remain legally accurate regardless of the period being assessed.•  Direct TaxPro Max compatibility: The generated PDF report is formatted to allow traders to directly transfer the figures into TaxPro Max for submission, completing the full compliance workflow from raw exchange data to a filed return.3.4 System DesignThe system design for Crypto-Tax NG is presented through Unified Modelling Language (UML) diagrams that capture the structural and behavioural characteristics of the proposed system. PostgreSQL was selected because of its robust support for decimal precision arithmetic, which is essential for computing tax figures to two decimal places of accuracy across large transaction volumes, its native support for the JSONField data type used to store raw CSV rows for audit trail purposes, and its proven compatibility with the Django ORM through the psycopg2 driver.
